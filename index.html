Origins → Practice → Direction

How I came to think in strategies.

Curiosity has been the throughline of my life—from dragging around an oversized atlas of the human body at six to crossing countries to study how minds and environments shape each other. I first trained in Occupational Therapy in India, where I saw how small design decisions in hospital rooms, homes, and communities could shift independence, recovery, and dignity.

That work left me with a question I couldn't shake: not just what people do, but why they do it within the systems they inherit. To answer it, I moved to the U.S. for a Master's in Applied Behavior Analysis and began treating campuses, classrooms, and platforms as living environments to be studied and re-designed.

I use the word strategy, not intervention, on purpose. An intervention is done to people; a strategy is built with them, into the systems they move through.

Bodies & environments

Occupational Therapy training in India taught me to read the relationship between a body and its environment: how furniture, lighting, and routines make function easier or harder.

From individuals to systems

Clinical work surfaced a pattern: individual “non-compliance” usually signaled a system that hadn't been designed for the real constraints people live with.

Research as infrastructure

Graduate research in sustainability and education let me design behavioral strategies into bins, signage, and digital tools—small infrastructural moves that produced measurable change.

Now: safety & AI systems

Today I audit AI systems and digital platforms using behavioral diagnostics, treating trust & safety policies like ecosystems that can be tuned rather than one-off rulebooks.

Selected work

Where strategies meet systems.

Campus sustainability

Designing recycling environments that do half the work.

Mixed-methods research on a university campus to redesign bin placement, signage, and prompts, improving recycling accuracy while supporting a zero-waste mandate.

17.4% improvement in recycling accuracy after the strategy package.

This work confirmed that most “awareness problems” are actually infrastructure problems.

AI & trust & safety

Auditing large language models with behavioral diagnostics.

Adapted an organizational performance checklist to probe LLM behavior across demographics and contexts, surfacing patterns of bias and failure modes.

Built a reusable framework for responsible AI evaluation that product, policy, and research teams can share.

I treat AI systems less like oracles and more like complex staff: trainable, fallible, and in need of clear feedback loops.

Digital safety for minors

Behavioral risks of hyper-saturated children's content.

Policy brief analyzing how highly saturated animated videos shape children's attention, regulation, and expectations of content on platforms.

Informed safety guidelines and education materials for teams working on child-centered online experiences.

Safety is most durable when it feels like care, not control.

Transit & climate

Using transit data to seed climate-aligned habits.

Ongoing SQL-based work with public transit data to design ridership incentives around major events, using behavioral economics to interpret patterns.

Exploring how subtle changes in timing, pricing, and messaging can tip choices toward lower-carbon options.

I'm interested in strategies that scale without becoming blunt instruments.

Field notes

What skies, streets, and signs taught me.

Looking up

I take a lot of photos of skies and planes. I like vantage points where you can see the whole system at once. Before I touch data or policy, I try to find this view.

Blue hour streets

I'm drawn to transitional states—rush hour, shifting norms, new tech. That's where behavior and systems are both in motion, and where small strategies can have outsized effects.

“Shoot your shot”

I don't believe in willpower as a strategy. I believe in environments that invite the right shot at the right time—online, on campus, or in a neighborhood.
